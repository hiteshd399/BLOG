The Beauty of the Unfinished Journey

A Reflection for Those Who Feel Lost, Yet Keep Walking — on purpose, cinema, and what it means to still be becoming.

As an IT student, I'm trained to think in binaries — right or wrong, true or false, success or failure. But life doesn't operate in clean logic. It's messy, unpredictable, and often illogical. We build systems, create routines, and try to impose order on chaos, only to realize that the most profound moments come from the things we can't control. The unexpected friendships. The failures that redirect us.

Life is not a problem to be solved, but an experience to be lived. We spend so much of our time chasing answers — What is my purpose? Why do I exist? What is the right path? — that we forget the simple truth: the search itself is the meaning.

We're taught that purpose is a singular, grand destination — a career, a mission, a legacy. But what if purpose isn't something we find, but something we cultivate through daily choices? A doctor saves lives, but so does the stranger who listens to a friend in despair. A scientist discovers new worlds, but so does the child who gazes at the stars for the first time.

Purpose isn't a fixed point. It's fluid, evolving as we do. It's in the small acts — showing up, paying attention, choosing kindness even when it costs us something. The myth of a single, grand purpose can paralyze us, making us feel like we're failing if we haven't "figured it out" by a certain age. But what if the real purpose was never about achieving something external, but about becoming someone who adds value to the world, in whatever form that takes?

"The people who inspire us the most aren't the ones who had everything figured out — but the ones who kept moving forward despite their doubts."

We're obsessed with completion — finishing degrees, securing jobs, checking off life milestones. But what if the most important parts of life are the unfinished ones? The dreams we haven't yet pursued, the questions we haven't answered, the person we're still becoming?

There's a certain grace in accepting that we may never have all the answers. That life isn't a puzzle to be solved, but a story to be written — one imperfect, uncertain chapter at a time.

Growing Up in Biratnagar — and How Cinema Became My First Friend

Growing up in Biratnagar until I was 19, I felt invisible — no friends, just the crushing weight of isolation that only deepened during lockdown, trapping me in a suffocating loop of loneliness. Until I discovered my lifelines: movies and music.

Films, especially Western cinema, became my sanctuary. My first true friends. And There Will Be Blood struck me like lightning — not just for its story, but for Daniel Day-Lewis's volcanic performance that laid bare the raw nerves of human obsession.

🎬
There Will Be Blood (2007)
Dir. Paul Thomas Anderson — the film that changed how I understood ambition, isolation, and what it means to be consumed by something.

In that darkness, cinema gave me what real life couldn't: a mirror for my own unspoken rage and longing, proof that art could articulate emotions I'd never been able to name. Those flickering images and haunting scores didn't just distract me — they resurrected me, teaching me that even in solitude, I was witnessing something profound about the human condition.

Now, every rewatch feels like visiting an old savior — a reminder that stories can be oxygen when the world refuses to breathe with you.

What Cinema Taught Me

Cinema is far more than just a source of entertainment — it's a profound reflection of the human experience that teaches us invaluable lessons about life. Every time we lose ourselves in a great film, we're not just escaping reality; we're engaging with perspectives and emotions that shape how we see the world.

Movies show us that everyone — from the protagonist to the seemingly insignificant side character — has a complex inner world worth understanding. Some of cinema's most powerful moments happen without words, proving that silence often carries more weight than dialogue.

Films expand our worldview, transporting us to places and situations we'd never encounter otherwise, putting our own struggles into perspective. Perhaps most importantly, movies demonstrate that real love and connection aren't about grand gestures, but about the small, consistent choices that bond people together.

"What began as escape has become my compass — because the right film at the right time doesn't just entertain; it whispers, 'You are not alone,' and that changes everything."

The magic of cinema lies in its ability to show us life's complexities in a condensed, meaningful way — giving us not just stories, but mirrors that help us understand ourselves and others more deeply. What makes films truly special is that while they have endings, our lives keep unfolding. And like the best movie characters, we're all still writing our stories, one day at a time.
